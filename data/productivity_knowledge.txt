THE EISENHOWER MATRIX
The Eisenhower Matrix divides tasks into four quadrants based on urgency and importance.
Quadrant 1: Urgent and Important - Do these tasks immediately. Examples include crises, deadlines, and emergencies.
Quadrant 2: Not Urgent but Important - Schedule these tasks. These are long-term goals, planning, and development activities.
Quadrant 3: Urgent but Not Important - Delegate these tasks if possible. These are interruptions and requests from others.
Quadrant 4: Not Urgent and Not Important - Eliminate or minimize these tasks. These are time wasters and distractions.
The key insight is that most people spend too much time in Quadrant 1 and 3, reacting to urgency rather than focusing on importance.

TIME BLOCKING
Time blocking means dedicating specific blocks of time to specific tasks or categories of work.
Benefits of time blocking include reduced context switching, better focus, realistic scheduling, and reduced decision fatigue.
How to time block: Identify your most important tasks. Assign each task a specific time window. Protect these blocks like appointments. Review and adjust daily.
Deep work blocks should be 90 to 120 minutes with no interruptions. Shallow tasks can be batched into shorter blocks.

DEEP WORK PRINCIPLES
Deep work means performing cognitively demanding tasks with full concentration and no distractions.
Deep work produces the most valuable output and builds rare skills quickly.
Rules for deep work: Schedule it in advance. Choose a consistent location. Set a clear finish time. Have a shutdown ritual. Remove all digital distractions.
The typical person can sustain about 4 hours of deep work per day. Beginners should start with 1 to 2 hours.

TASK BATCHING
Task batching means grouping similar tasks together to reduce cognitive switching costs.
Examples of batching: Reply to all emails at 9am and 4pm only. Make all phone calls in one block. Do all creative work in the morning. Do all administrative work in the afternoon.
Batching reduces startup cost of each task and improves momentum and flow.

EXAM PREPARATION STRATEGIES
Effective exam preparation requires reverse planning from the exam date.
Steps: Identify exam date. List all topics to cover. Estimate hours needed per topic. Divide across available days. Add buffer days for review.
Prioritize topics by weight in the exam. High-weight topics deserve more time.
Active recall beats passive reading. Use flashcards, practice tests, and self-quizzing.
Spaced repetition means reviewing material at increasing intervals to strengthen memory.
Avoid cramming. Distributed practice over many sessions is far more effective.
During exam week: reduce new learning and focus on review and practice.

ASSIGNMENT PLANNING LOGIC
For any assignment, break the work into phases: research, outline, draft, revise, submit.
Start the assignment the day it is assigned, even for just 10 minutes. This reduces procrastination and activates subconscious processing.
Work backwards from the deadline. If deadline is Friday, aim to finish by Wednesday to allow revision.
If the assignment requires research, allocate at least 30% of total time to research.
Always leave buffer time for unexpected issues like technical problems or missing information.

INTERVIEW PREPARATION SEQUENCING
Interview preparation should be sequenced in this order:
1. Research the company and role thoroughly.
2. Review your resume and prepare stories for each point.
3. Practice common behavioral questions using STAR format.
4. Prepare technical or role-specific content.
5. Do mock interviews or out-loud practice.
6. Prepare your questions for the interviewer.
7. Plan logistics: outfit, route, timing.
Complete company research first because it informs all other preparation.
Do mock interviews last when you have the content ready to practice.

BURNOUT AND OVERLOAD SIGNALS
Signs of cognitive overload: difficulty concentrating, forgetting simple things, feeling overwhelmed by normal tasks.
Signs of schedule overload: more tasks than available hours, multiple deadlines on same day, no buffer time, no breaks.
Prevention strategies: Limit to 3 major tasks per day. Include transition time between tasks. Build in at least one rest block. Say no to non-essential additions.
When overloaded, first step is triage: identify what can be dropped, delegated, or delayed without major consequence.

DEADLINE TRIAGE STRATEGIES
When multiple deadlines compete, apply this triage logic:
First, identify hard deadlines (external, fixed, consequences if missed) versus soft deadlines (self-imposed or flexible).
Second, identify dependencies: which tasks block other tasks?
Third, identify reversibility: can a deadline be extended by asking? If yes, it becomes lower priority.
Hard deadlines with high consequences must be treated as Quadrant 1 regardless of how they feel.
Tasks with downstream dependencies should be moved up in priority to unblock other work.

PROCRASTINATION REDUCTION TACTICS
The two-minute rule: if a task takes less than two minutes, do it immediately instead of scheduling it.
Implementation intentions: plan exactly when, where, and how you will do a task. Reduces decision overhead at execution time.
Temptation bundling: pair unpleasant tasks with enjoyable activities.
Make the next action specific and physical. Not "work on project" but "open file and write first paragraph."
Reduce friction: keep tools ready, workspace prepared, and context loaded before starting.

EXECUTION PRIORITIZATION RULES
Rule 1: Do the most important task first, before checking email or messages.
Rule 2: Never end a work session without deciding what the first task of the next session will be.
Rule 3: Single-task one item to completion before starting the next.
Rule 4: Protect deep work hours. Respond to messages in batches.
Rule 5: Review tomorrow's plan tonight so you wake up with a clear agenda.
Rule 6: When in doubt about what to do next, choose the task with the earliest real deadline.
Rule 7: Incomplete tasks take up mental space. Close open loops by either doing, delegating, or deferring with a date.

PRIORITY SCORING LOGIC
When scoring task priority, consider these factors:
Deadline proximity: tasks due today or tomorrow score highest.
Consequence of delay: missing some deadlines has high cost (failed exam, missed job opportunity) while others have low cost.
Effort versus time available: a high-effort task needs to start early even if deadline is not today.
Dependency: tasks that block other tasks should be elevated regardless of their own deadline.
External dependency: tasks that require other people to respond should be started first because of wait time.
Urgency is not the same as importance. Urgency is time pressure. Importance is long-term value.

STUDENT PRODUCTIVITY FRAMEWORK
Students should plan their week every Sunday evening.
Categorize all tasks by subject and type: lecture review, assignment, reading, exam prep.
Prioritize assignments with hard deadlines above readings and reviews.
Study difficult subjects when energy is highest, usually morning.
Use Pomodoro technique: 25 minutes focus, 5 minutes break.
Never skip sleep to study. Sleep consolidates memory. A rested brain learns faster.
Space study sessions across days rather than cramming in one long session.

WORK MODE PRODUCTIVITY
In work mode, start each day by reviewing email only after completing the first priority task.
Use calendar blocking for meetings and deep work.
After each meeting, immediately capture action items before moving on.
At end of day, do a 5-minute shutdown ritual: close open loops, write tomorrow's first task, clear desk.
Protect the first 90 minutes of the workday as sacred focus time.
Respond to messages in batches: once at morning, once at afternoon.

REALISTIC DAILY PLANNING
A realistic workday has capacity for 4 to 6 hours of actual focused work.
Meetings, breaks, transitions, and unexpected issues consume 2 to 4 hours of the typical 8-hour workday.
Never plan more than 3 high-effort tasks in one day.
Always include buffer time of at least 30 minutes for unexpected issues.
Plan for energy, not just time. Morning is for complex tasks. Afternoon for meetings and reviews. Evening for light tasks.

OVERLOAD BALANCING TACTICS
When you have too many tasks for the available time:
Step 1: Separate hard deadlines from soft deadlines.
Step 2: Identify the 3 most critical tasks that must be done today.
Step 3: Move all others to tomorrow or later in the week.
Step 4: If deadline cannot be moved, look for tasks to drop or delegate.
Step 5: Communicate early if a deadline is at risk. Do not wait until the last moment.
Trying to do everything when overloaded leads to everything being done poorly.

PERSONAL PRODUCTIVITY FUNDAMENTALS
Physical tasks like grocery shopping and errands should be batched geographically or by location.
Administrative tasks like renewals, forms, and appointments should be handled in a dedicated block once a week.
Personal wellbeing tasks like exercise and rest are not optional. They enable all other tasks.
A daily planning habit of 10 minutes each morning or evening compounds significantly over time.
